Journey

I left school in 2017 with big dreams — founded on very little research — of starting a development thinktank. It was white-saviour-y and problematic. I promise I've changed.

I enlisted and served in the military between 2018 and 2019. I'm not Singaporean, so I approached conscription as a voluntary exercise I would "grow from". Many nights I regretted this while cracking a chocolate biscuit in the jungle and praying the next day would be better — but I'm proud of this period of my life. I overcame a collarbone break and commissioned as an Infantry Officer. I also detonated C4, averted a hand-grenade accident, and learnt to sleep anywhere.

Towards the end of my time in the military, I wrote to poverty labs and development thinktanks looking for an internship. All 20 said something to the effect of: "you don't have a degree, forget about it." So I decided to volunteer somewhere instead while working odd jobs to save up.

In 2020, I spent 6 months volunteering in Moria Camp, Greece, with Movement on the Ground. I was only meant to stay three months — but when COVID got bad and other volunteers left the island, I felt a lot of calm in my decision to stay. That decision changed my life. I grew close to my tent-building team, who taught me Farsi and loved me in a way I still think about today. I cried when I got home and lay in my own bed. It wasn't fair that I could leave whenever I wanted, but my friends were still there — and indefinitely there. I knew I couldn't go about my life forgetting what I had seen, and I wrote about these months here.

I began a law degree at Oxford towards the end of 2020. I knew I didn't want to practise, but was conned by the idea of "transferable skills" — which I'm still scratching my head about today. Nonetheless, Oxford was a blessing. Besides the people and the beautiful Port Meadow, there were opportunities to pursue my interests. I built my own curriculum (attending more international development lectures than law ones), joined a refugee health start-up, and founded a student thinktank helping grassroots NGOs with research pieces. My college even paid for me to go back to the Greek camps for four months, as well as to do research in Kenya and Uganda on migrant livelihoods with the Global Development Incubator.

After somehow graduating, I moved to Beijing to enrol as a Schwarzman Scholar at Tsinghua University. The program was very international, and being in that environment humbled me — I struggled to navigate friendships and found myself worrying about how I stacked up against my peers. During the Chinese New Year break I backpacked through Sichuan, Chongqing, and Yunnan to research China's Poverty Alleviation Resettlement program — a planned relocation project that has moved over 10 million people. It was riveting to dive into ethnographic research this way, though also riveting in the sense that I was being tracked by a local police unit in Yilong Xian for the entirety of my fieldwork. My research was limited by the fact that I speak Chinese like a 12-year-old, on a good day.

At the end of my year in China, I was fortunate to join People's Courage International's Innovation Team as a Strategy Associate, based in Bengaluru. I designed pilot programs for agricultural communities in Indonesia — which entailed a lot of travelling to rice farms in the middle of Java, asking questions like "Could you tell me about the last time your village had to deal with a drought?" The role taught me that I was good at asking questions and putting pieces together, but had some way to go in terms of seeing things move from proposal to action.

It was during this period, doing fieldwork in Madhya Pradesh, that I visited a village experiencing a severe drought where residents were unable to grow anything. I thought: surely in this day and age there are urban farming techniques that mean this doesn't have to be the case. I spent the next few months researching obsessively. I remembered a doctor I'd met in Greece who shared that 90% of the medical issues she treated from the camp were due to bad diet — and I began wondering whether urban farming had some answers.

In July 2025, I left PCI and found myself in Samos, Greece, building a microgreens farm in a refugee-supporting community centre — the beginning of my non-profit, From Seed to Shelter. The farm remains in operation through La Maison, and I submitted results to the WFP with whom I'm exploring how to scale this to relevant contexts. Moving forward, I'm shifting focus somewhat from dietary diversity towards more straightforward food security.

Towards the end of 2025, a side-project became more serious: my card game, 100% Disagree. I travelled across the US pitching it on college campuses. A card game company had told me they liked the concept but I'd need to demonstrate "viral potential" — and as someone who doesn't use social media, I got a very quick education in that world. I posted a video every day and tried to build an audience. I managed 340 followers in 20 days before the feedback I was getting meant the game was no longer recognisable. I eventually found myself in conversation with a large brand for a couple of months — though this ultimately fell through. I'm still hoping to get it published. I like to think it's my way of trying to create space for silly in the world.

Most recently, in 2026 I started as an Entrepreneur in Residence at Raidical Academy by Triple A Ventures. It has been eye-opening — I'm the only person at the final stage without any business experience whatsoever, and so I'm asking questions like "what's a moat?" and "what's a TAM?" all the time. I can't share too much detail about what I'm building, but I'm hoping something will emerge that could fund the humanitarian problem-solving I want to keep doing, without the constant financial pressure. Even if that never materialises, I'm already grateful for what I'm learning just from speaking to investors and understanding what customer discovery looks like.
